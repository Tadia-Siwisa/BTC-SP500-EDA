Post 1: Visuals Can Deceive

It’s common practice to compare Bitcoin and the S&P 500 on one chart. I didn’t know that until I tried it myself. Then I realized: wait, this doesn’t seem right. Top panel: Two honest scales* Bitcoin: $15k to $45k. S&P 500: 3800 to 4800 points. Clean data. Honest scales. But you can’t compare dollars to index points. No conclusion possible. Bottom panel: Dual-axis overlay One chart. Two lines. One story. Looks correlated, right? That’s the trap. Your eyes lie before your brain reads the axes. Before you notice left = points, right = dollars... Your brain already decided: “these move together”. The deception happens before you think. $40,000 looks like 4800 points because I chose the scales to make it look that way. Here’s what I learned: Price tells you direction. It doesn’t tell you conviction. If price is the what, volume is the why. If RSI is the temperature, volume is the heartbeat. Here’s what’s coming - I break 2023 into 3 specific windows. Not “the year”, but the moments. -Weekend moves. Because Bitcoin trades 24/7 and stocks don’t. -The 3-way view. Price + RSI + Volume side by side. No forced scales. Just moments where money actually moves. I stopped comparing prices. Next post: I start comparing moments.

Post 2: Correlation Depends on Perspective

Let's talk about correlation. What is it? One number that averages how 2 assets moved together over time. 2023 BTC vs S&P 500: month to month, correlation swung from -0.81 to 0.85. Same year, same two assets, flipping from strongly negative to strongly positive within months. Here's the nuance most posts skip: this doesn't mean the relationship is fake. Bitcoin and the S&P 500 are both risk assets, both sensitive to liquidity conditions and Fed policy. That's a real, structural link. But "real" and "stable" aren't the same thing. The link is genuine and the relationship is noisy, both at once. A single correlation number averages away exactly the part that matters: when the relationship holds, when it breaks, and why. So let's look deeper with Moments of Interest. For this post: March 2023. Correlation that month: ∼0.30. Positive, but nowhere near the extremes either side of it. Why March? Banking crisis. SVB, Signature, Credit Suisse. One of the months with several volume spikes above the 95th percentile, the days traders actually cared. To see why, we need to look at March the right way. In post one of this series, I made the case against plotting BTC and the S&P on the same dual-axis chart, two indices with completely different scales forced onto one set of axes, which distorts how their movements actually compare. Here I'm pushing that further: instead of one overlaid line chart, or RSI and volume on their own, look at price, RSI, and volume stacked together, side by side, for both indices. Three signals instead of one, run in parallel rather than layered. That's what image 2 is doing, and it shows what a single price chart never could: 1. *Difference in time*: Banks collapsed Friday 4pm. BTC traded all weekend. Stocks waited till Monday. You only see that gap when price is lined up next to volume, day by day. 2. *Different incentives*: Same headline, opposite trades. BTC RSI pushed toward overbought as capital looked for an alternative. S&P RSI ground toward oversold as equity holders priced in contagion risk. Price alone shows the result. RSI next to it shows the reasoning. None of this changes when you zoom out. It changes when you reorganize. Same data, laid out differently, and a different story comes forward. That's the thread running through this whole series: not just correlation, but the habit of looking again at the way we're shown information in the first place, because the structure of a chart decides what you're allowed to see.

Post 3: Changing the View Changes the Questions

In my first post, I learned that charts can mislead. In the second, I learned that correlation depends on where you look. This time, I learned something about analysis itself. When I zoomed into June 2023, I thought I was looking for an explanation. Instead, I found better questions. A price chart tells you what happened. Bitcoin fell after the SEC lawsuits against Binance and Coinbase. Bitcoin recovered after BlackRock filed for a spot ETF. Simple. But then I added RSI. Now I could see momentum. Then I added volume. Now I could see participation. And suddenly the story became more complicated. The chart stopped giving answers and started generating questions. - Did momentum begin changing before the move became obvious? - Which days attracted the most participation? - Why did the largest volume spike occur after the ETF filing? - Were markets reacting to the news, or were they already repositioning beforehand? Price tells you what happened. RSI tells you how momentum changed. Volume tells you where market attention concentrated. Viewed together, the same month starts telling a different story. The biggest lesson from this project wasn't about Bitcoin. It was about perspective. Every time I changed the view, my interpretation changed. And that's what makes analysis interesting. Not finding answers. Learning how to see the same information from a different perspective. Because better questions don't come from looking harder. They come from looking differently.
